404 Error messages

The purpose of 404 error messages

404 error messages inform users that the page they’re trying to reach can’t be found. This typically happens due to broken links, changed URLs, deleted pages, or mistyped addresses.

In an ideal world, users should rarely see a 404 page, but when they do, it plays a crucial role. If someone arrives from a Google search or an external link and only finds an error, it could end their journey on your website unless the 404 page helps them to resolve the problem or redirects them somewhere where they can find help. However, the chances that users will explore other pages on a platform after seeing the 404 error message are close to zero.

These 404 error pages tell a lot about the company. They not only show how well you are or aren’t prepared for potential problems but, most importantly, how you treat users when they encounter a problem on your website or within your software.

Recommendations and best practices

The examples above prove that 404 error messages don’t have to be scary. You can include funny pictures, animations, or graphics that will turn this potentially negative experience into something positive. Moreover, try to write an explanation using your brand’s voice and tone so that users know why the page is missing. Well-crafted 404 error messages are a good chance to show empathy, apologize, and reflect users’ feelings.

The table below presents recommendations and best practices for the key aspects of 404 error messages.

404 error messages Recommendations and best practices
Title

  • Use a friendly, human tone.

  • Avoid robotic or generic phrases like “Error 404”.

  • Avoid using exclamations.
Body

  • Explain the issue without using technical language. Try to be creative and prepare a message that represents your brand.

  • Include images or animations.

  • Be transparent and helpful.
Call to action (CTA)

  • Offer practical solutions such as navigating to the home page.

  • If applicable, provide alternative solutions or suggest contacting support.
Accessibility

  • Don’t use jargon.

  • If there are any links needed, make them as descriptive as possible.

  • Provide descriptive alt text for any visuals or illustrations.
