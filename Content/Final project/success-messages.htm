Success messages

The purpose of success messages

Users use software products to accomplish specific goals. They need to stay informed whether they’re getting closer to achieving that goal.

A success message appears after a user completes an action or task. This message confirms that a specific step has been completed without any issues and is one of the most important responses that the user expects to receive. Moreover, success messages help users feel more confident in their interactions with software and improve the user experience.

What is important, success messages not only inform that everything is okay, but also instruct users by telling them about the next optional or mandatory steps. Many companies go even further with creating success messages and want to add extra meaning to the action and leave users with a positive feeling about their brand. This is why so many companies publish and strictly follow their UX guides. See the Principles of clear and effective UI messages chapter to check the list of publicly available UX guides.

The list below presents examples of actions that conclude with a success message:

  • Purchasing a product

  • Signing up for a newsletter

  • Unsubscribing

  • Sending a contact form

  • Importing or exporting data

  • Validating email address

  • Opting in for a free trial

  • Recovering a password

Recommendations and best practices

The examples above show that success messages serve various purposes. When writing them, always keep the user in mind and ensure the messages are clear and specific. If an action is required, make it explicit. If the goal is just to confirm a completed action, avoid repeating the same information in the title and in the body of the message. Don’t be too descriptive if it’s not needed.

The table below presents recommendations and best practices for the key aspects of success messages.

Success messages Recommendations and best practices
Title

  • Use titles only when needed.

  • Avoid adding titles that convey the same message as the text below the title.

  • Avoid using exclamations.
Body

  • Include the reason for the success - be informative about what actions a user has completed.

  • Don’t write more than 2 sentences.

  • Avoid including too technical information as people may overlook it.

Call to action (CTA)

  • CTAs are usually optional in success messages unless it’s important to take action.

  • Always give a user an option to dismiss the message.

  • Use as few words for CTA as possible.
Accessibility

  • Don’t use jargon.

  • If there are any links needed, make them as descriptive as possible.

  • Make text easily scannable to focus the user’s attention on key information.
